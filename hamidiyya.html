Al-Hamidiyya Souq – The Beating Heart of Damascus

Al-Hamidiyya Souq is the most famous covered market in Damascus and one of the great bazaars of the Arab world. Stretching over 600 metres through the heart of the Old City, it was built in the late 19th century during the reign of Ottoman Sultan Abdülhamid II, after whom it is named. Its grand arched iron roof — riddled with bullet holes from the French Mandate period — is one of the most photographed sights in Syria.

The souq begins at the grand entrance gate near Bab al-Nasr and leads directly to the western gate of the Umayyad Mosque, passing hundreds of shops selling textiles, gold jewellery, spices, Damascene brocade (damask fabric), antiques, souvenir glassware, and handmade crafts. At the far end, you emerge blinking into the sunlight of the mosque's outer courtyard.

Beyond shopping, the souq is a sensory and social experience. The smell of spices drifts from the spice traders' stalls. Tea vendors push trolleys through the crowd. Artisans demonstrate woodwork inlaid with mother-of-pearl. And the bullet-hole-pierced roof filters the afternoon sun into shafts of golden light — one of the great unexpected visual moments of a Damascus visit.

Highlights of Al-Hamidiyya Souq

19th Century

The Iron Roof

The souq's most iconic feature — a barrel-vaulted iron roof pierced with bullet holes from the French Mandate bombing of 1925. On sunny afternoons, shafts of light pierce through the holes, creating a magical diffused atmosphere inside.

Living Craft

Damascene Crafts & Brocade

The souq is the best place in Syria to find authentic Damascene brocade (damask), hand-inlaid wood with mother-of-pearl, copper and brass work, traditional glassware, and locally produced rose water and soaps.

Food & Spice

Spice & Food Stalls

At the edges of the souq, the spice traders' stalls overflow with za'atar, sumac, cinnamon bark, dried rosebuds, and Syrian coffee blends. Try freshly squeezed pomegranate juice or stop at a street stall for a warm knafeh from the oven.
