The Ancient City of Canatha

Kanawat — ancient Canatha — is one of the most remarkable open-air archaeological sites in the Middle East. Perched on the lava-rock slopes of the Jebel al-Arab (Hauran mountain), it was one of the ten cities of the Decapolis, the league of Greco-Roman cities that flourished under Roman rule.

The site contains a stunning array of well-preserved monuments: a Nabataean temple from the 1st century BC, the magnificent Temple of Zeus with its towering columns, a Byzantine basilica church built over a Roman Serapeion, and a large nymphaeum (monumental fountain). Walking through Kanawat is a journey across at least a dozen civilizations.

Despite its extraordinary preservation, Kanawat receives only a fraction of the visitors that flock to Petra or Palmyra — making it a rare opportunity to experience ancient history in near-solitude.

Highlights of Kanawat

Major monuments and must-see sites

Roman Period

Temple of Zeus

The largest monument in Kanawat, dedicated to Jupiter/Zeus. Several towering basalt columns still stand, framing views of the surrounding countryside.

2nd Century AD

Serapeion & Basilica

A remarkable palimpsest of history: a Roman temple to Serapis later converted into a Byzantine basilica church. Both structures coexist beautifully on the same site.

Nabataean Period

Nabataean Temple

Predating the Roman conquest, this 1st century BC temple was built by the Nabataeans and later adopted by Roman settlers. Fine carved stonework still visible.
